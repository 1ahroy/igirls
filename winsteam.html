Women In STEAM


Peseshet


Peseshet is often credited with being the first female physician of ancient Egypt nearly 5,000 years ago. While historical documents remain unclear and her existence is often debated, she represents the significance of female physicians in society and how women have always been leading figures within STEAM.


Ada Lovelace

“The First Programmer”

Born: December 10, 1815
Died: November 27, 1852

In 1843, Ada Lovelace published a translation of an Italian engineer’s work called Analytical Engines - she added her own notes to this translation, including an algorithm sequence for solving certain mathematical problems. It is through this work that she is known as computer science’s “First Programmer”.


Dr. Dorothy Lavinia Brown


Born: January 7, 1919
Died: June 13, 2004

Spending her childhood in orphanages, Brown grew up to become the first African American female surgeon in the South in 1948. In 1957, she was appointed as Chief of Surgery at Nashville’s Riverside Hospital. In addition to creating a hugely successful career in medicine, Brown also found political success. She became the first single woman to adopt a child in 1956 and became the first black woman elected as a Representative for Tennessee.


Dr. Gagandeep Kang


Born: November 3, 1962

Dr. Kang became the first Indian woman to be inducted into The Royal Society, one of the most prestigious scientific academies in the world, in its 360 year history. She is a microbiologist and virologist, who specializes in viral infection transmission in children.


Sabrina Gonsalez Paterski


Born: June 3, 1993

Paterski is a renowned American theoretical physicist who is currently studying high energy physics. A first-generation Cuban-American, she became the youngest person ever to fly their own airplane at age 14. After receiving her undergraduate degree, she went on to study at MIT for three years, earning the highest possible GPA of 5.0. She received her PhD from Harvard University and is ranked among the likes of Stephen Hawking in scientific innovation and creativity.
